My Story

Dirt under her fingernails, since always.

From a grandfather's garden, to eleven years leading engineering teams, to a leap into landscape design.

Gemma grew up with dirt under her fingernails. Her grandfather's garden wound through his yard with paths and fish ponds and more plant varieties than she could name. Her father built his own stone walls, brick patios, and sheds — and gave her a small plot within his own garden to tend however she liked. Her first jobs were weeding, paid by the hour. Plants have been part of her life ever since.

For the past eleven years, Gemma built a career as a senior software engineer and engineering manager, mostly at Spotify — leading teams of up to sixteen engineers and shipping products used by hundreds of millions of people. She's managed complex, cross-functional projects from inception through launch, driven strategy, and delivered under real stakes. She's good at it — but it was never where she felt most alive.

She woke up those mornings buzzing. She felt competent, grounded, and genuinely proud of the work.

Last year, she left that career intentionally — not because it wasn't going well, but because she wanted to find work that felt more like her. She suspected gardening might be it. To test that suspicion, she took on her first client project last spring: transforming a difficult post-tree-stump site in Arlington into a layered perennial garden, complete with a fieldstone retaining wall she built herself. She woke up those mornings buzzing. She felt competent, grounded, and genuinely proud of the work. The suspicion was confirmed.

Gemma's taking the leap into landscape design deliberately and with both eyes open — bringing the project management discipline and attention to detail of a tech career into the craft of landscape design and fine gardening. Her particular focus is on edible perennials integrated into ornamental gardens, native plants, and spaces that feel abundant and alive rather than manicured and static.

Gemma lives in East Arlington with her family, where her garden — the thing she's most proud of having made — is always a work in progress.
